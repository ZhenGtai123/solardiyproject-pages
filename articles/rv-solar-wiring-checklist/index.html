RV Solar Wiring Checklist: Fuses, Disconnects, and Cable Routes

A practical RV solar wiring checklist for small systems, focused on cable routes, fuse placement, controller current, and service access.

RV solar wiring has to survive vibration, tight spaces, changing weather, and owners who eventually add one more device. A system can be correctly sized on paper and still feel unreliable if the wiring plan is treated as an afterthought.

This checklist is for small RV solar systems: panels, charge controller, battery, low-voltage distribution, and optional inverter. It is not a substitute for electrical code, product manuals, or professional help when high current or shore-power integration is involved.

1. Map the current path

Before choosing cable or connectors, draw the path current will follow:

solar panel -> controller -> battery -> fuse block or inverter -> load -> return path

Do not forget the return path. Voltage drop and heat depend on the full circuit length, not only the distance from the battery to the load. If the battery is 10 feet from a device, the electrical path can be close to 20 feet.

2. Put fuses near the source

The battery is the source that can deliver the most fault current. A fuse should protect the cable, not merely the device at the far end. That usually means the main battery fuse belongs close to the battery positive terminal, before the cable travels through cabinets or compartments.

Solar input protection depends on array size, controller instructions, and panel configuration. The key is to read the equipment manual and leave space for the protection hardware instead of trying to add it after every surface is full.

3. Choose controller current with margin

A charge controller should be sized for the array and system voltage. A simple planning estimate is:

array watts / battery voltage x safety margin

For example, a 400 W array into a 12 V battery can exceed 30 A before margin. That does not automatically mean a 30 A controller is enough. Temperature, panel behavior, and manufacturer limits matter. Use the controller manual as the final authority.

4. Keep inverter cables short

Inverters draw high current from low-voltage batteries. A 600 W AC load can require roughly 50 A from a 12 V battery before losses. That is why inverter cables are usually short, thick, fused, and routed carefully.

If the inverter must be far from the battery, ask whether the inverter location is wrong. Moving AC wiring after the inverter is often easier than moving high-current DC wiring before it.

5. Avoid hidden service traps

RV interiors make it tempting to hide everything. Hidden wiring can look clean, but it creates problems when a fuse trips, a connector loosens, or a battery needs inspection.

Useful service details include:

  • Accessible fuses and breakers.
  • Cable labels at both ends.
  • Strain relief where cables enter boxes or compartments.
  • Protected edges where wire passes through metal or wood.
  • Enough slack for inspection without pulling on terminals.

The goal is not a showroom. The goal is a system you can inspect without dismantling half the RV.

6. Separate planning from final torque

The layout stage is for routing, measuring, and verifying. The final build stage is for correct lugs, torque, strain relief, and testing. Do not treat finger-tight mockups as finished work.

After final assembly, check voltage under load. A connection can look fine at rest and still drop voltage when the inverter or pump starts.

Quick pre-power checklist

Before energizing the system, confirm:

  • Battery polarity is correct.
  • Main fuse is installed in the correct location.
  • Controller battery connection order follows the manual.
  • PV disconnect is reachable.
  • Cables are protected from sharp edges and movement.
  • Wire gauge matches expected current and length.
  • Nothing depends on a cable being pulled tight.

Good RV solar wiring is quiet. It does not buzz, heat, flicker, or require guessing. A careful checklist turns a pile of parts into a system that can be maintained on the road.
