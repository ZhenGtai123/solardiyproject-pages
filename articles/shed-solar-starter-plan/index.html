A Practical Starter Plan for a Shed Solar System

A step-by-step planning guide for a small shed solar project, from loads and battery size to panel wattage and wiring boundaries.

Building solar for a shed looks simple until the shopping cart starts filling with mismatched parts. A panel, a battery, a charge controller, an inverter, some wire, and a fuse block are not automatically a system. A good plan starts with the jobs the shed must perform, then turns those jobs into watt-hours, current, protection, and mounting decisions.

This guide covers a small off-grid shed system for lighting, tool charging, a router, a camera, or occasional laptop use. It is not a grid-tie guide. If the project connects to a home panel, touches utility power, involves roof work, or uses high-current battery banks, bring in a qualified electrician and follow local code.

Start with loads

Write every device in a load table. The important number is not just watts; it is watts multiplied by hours.

Load Watts Hours per day Daily watt-hours
LED lights 20 W 3 h 60 Wh
Camera and router 18 W 24 h 432 Wh
Laptop charging 65 W 2 h 130 Wh
Tool charger 120 W 0.5 h 60 Wh

This example lands near 682 Wh per day. Add a planning buffer for cold weather, cloudy days, inverter losses, and the fact that people often add one more device later. A 25 percent buffer moves the target to about 850 Wh per day.

Decide whether you need an inverter

The inverter is often the quiet efficiency problem. If your shed only needs DC lights, USB charging, a camera, and a router, a 12 V or 24 V DC system can avoid conversion losses. If you need AC outlets, choose an inverter sized for the actual loads, not for an imaginary future workshop.

For small systems, a 300 W to 600 W pure sine wave inverter is often more sensible than a 2000 W unit. A large inverter may have higher idle consumption, thicker wiring requirements, and larger fuse needs. The best inverter is the smallest one that safely handles the loads you truly plan to run.

Pick a battery target

Battery capacity should cover daily load and the number of cloudy days you want to survive. A simple first estimate is:

daily Wh x days of autonomy / usable battery fraction

If the shed uses 850 Wh per day and you want one day of autonomy, a LiFePO4 battery used to 80 percent depth of discharge would need:

850 Wh / 0.8 = 1063 Wh

That points toward a 12 V 100 Ah LiFePO4 battery, which is roughly 1280 Wh nominal. If the shed has 24-hour networking or security loads, consider two days of autonomy or a larger panel array.

Size the solar array

Panel sizing depends on daily watt-hours, peak sun hours, shading, and system losses. If the site gets 4.5 peak sun hours and you need 850 Wh per day, a rough array is:

850 Wh / 4.5 h / 0.75 = 252 W

Add a buffer and a 300 W to 400 W array becomes more comfortable. For a shed roof with partial shade, ground mounting may outperform a roof mount even if it looks less tidy.

Keep wiring conservative

Low-voltage systems carry more current than many beginners expect. A 600 W inverter on a 12 V battery can pull around 50 amps before losses. That is not thin speaker wire territory. Fuses, disconnects, wire gauge, lugs, strain relief, and battery terminal protection matter.

Use a voltage drop calculator before buying copper. Try to keep critical DC runs near 3 percent drop or less. Shorter wires, higher system voltage, and larger wire all help.

A reasonable starter parts list

A modest shed system might include:

  • 300 W to 400 W of solar panels
  • MPPT charge controller rated for the panel voltage and battery current
  • 12 V 100 Ah LiFePO4 battery with a reputable BMS
  • Small pure sine inverter if AC is required
  • DC fuse block for lights and USB outlets
  • Correctly sized fuses or breakers near the battery
  • PV disconnect, battery disconnect, lugs, conduit, and strain relief

Do not treat this as a universal shopping list. It is a shape. Your final list depends on loads, wire lengths, climate, mounting, and local rules.

Final check

A shed solar project works best when it is planned like a small electrical system, not like a gadget bundle. Load table first, battery second, array third, wiring and protection always. When the numbers are visible, the system becomes easier to build, troubleshoot, and upgrade.
