Battery Runtime Math: Why Nameplate Watt-Hours Are Not the Whole Story

Learn how depth of discharge, inverter efficiency, idle draw, and battery chemistry affect real runtime in small solar systems.

Battery runtime is one of the most misunderstood numbers in DIY solar. A battery labeled 1280 Wh does not mean every connected device receives exactly 1280 Wh of useful energy. Real runtime depends on chemistry, discharge limits, inverter efficiency, cable losses, temperature, and how steady the load is.

The goal is not perfect prediction. The goal is a planning number conservative enough to keep a small system from being undersized.

The basic formula

The simple runtime estimate is:

battery watt-hours x usable fraction x conversion efficiency / load watts

A 1280 Wh battery powering a 120 W AC load through a 90 percent efficient inverter with an 80 percent usable depth of discharge gives:

1280 x 0.8 x 0.9 / 120 = 7.68 hours

That number is already lower than the nameplate capacity suggests. For planning, it is reasonable to apply another small reserve so the system is not designed to run at the edge every day.

Depth of discharge matters

Lead-acid and LiFePO4 batteries behave very differently. Many lead-acid systems are planned around shallower discharge because deep cycling shortens life. LiFePO4 can usually use more capacity, but the battery management system, temperature, current limits, and manufacturer guidance still matter.

Do not assume that using 100 percent of capacity is wise. Even when the chemistry allows deep discharge, leaving reserve reduces nuisance shutdowns and gives the system margin for cold weather or aging.

Inverters have two costs

An inverter has conversion loss and idle draw. Conversion loss happens while powering AC loads. Idle draw happens when the inverter is on, even if the device connected to it is doing nothing.

For example, a small router that needs 12 W AC might become a 14 W battery load after inverter loss. If the inverter also consumes 8 W at idle, the system may spend almost as much energy keeping the inverter awake as it spends running the router. For always-on low-power devices, DC power can be a major improvement.

Load shape changes the answer

A refrigerator, pump, tool charger, and laptop do not behave like a steady resistor. Some loads surge, cycle, or taper. A battery may handle average watts but still hit current limits during startup. That is why runtime planning and inverter sizing are related but not identical.

If a load has a motor, compressor, heating element, or charger brick, check both running watts and surge or maximum input watts. A runtime calculator tells you energy duration; it does not guarantee that the inverter can start the load.

Temperature is not a footnote

Cold temperatures can reduce available capacity, especially for lead-acid batteries. LiFePO4 batteries also require care when charging near or below freezing unless they include low-temperature charging protection or internal heating.

For an outdoor shed or unconditioned cabin, battery placement can matter as much as battery size. A warm, protected, ventilated, code-compliant location can improve both performance and life.

A conservative planning habit

After estimating runtime, ask three questions:

  • What happens if the load runs 20 percent longer?
  • What happens if solar production is low tomorrow?
  • What happens if the battery has aged after a few years?

If the answer to any question is “the system fails immediately,” the design needs more capacity, lower loads, better charging, or a clearer usage rule.

Battery math is not there to make a build complicated. It prevents disappointment. When a runtime number includes usable capacity, efficiency, reserve, and real load behavior, the final system feels calmer because fewer surprises are hiding in the wiring.
